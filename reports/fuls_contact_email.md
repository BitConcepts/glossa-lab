**About the research project**

I am building an open-source computational platform — Glossa Lab — for the
structural analysis of undeciphered writing systems. The platform implements
structure-first, corpus-first methodology: recovering latent sign classes,
positional constraints, and candidate degrees of freedom before any phonetic
hypotheses are tested.

For the Indus script specifically, I am following a rigorous nine-phase workflow:

1. Multi-source corpus ingestion with full provenance metadata
2. Canonical sign registry and cross-system sign crosswalk
3. Non-destructive sequence normalization
4. Frequency, positional, n-gram, and graph structural analysis
5. Latent sign class discovery (positional behavior clustering)
6. Candidate degree-of-freedom schema estimation
7. Only then: controlled linguistic hypothesis testing

I am explicitly NOT pursuing premature phonetic assignments. The goal is a
reproducible structural baseline that can inform future decipherment work.

**Current data situation**

I have ingested the following open-access sources so far:

- mayig/indus-valley-script-corpus (MIT license, 184 Mohenjo-daro inscriptions,
  Parpola sign numbering)
- Yajnadevam indus-website SQL corpus (GPL-3.0, 2,543 multi-site inscriptions
  across 52 sites including Harappa, Dholavira, Lothal, Kalibangan, and others,
  Yajnadevam GLYPHID numbering)
- Mahadevan (1977) — partial OCR only

The Yajnadevam corpus uses its own sign numbering system, and I am currently
building a crosswalk between Yajnadevam GLYPHIDs and Parpola P-numbers via
sequence alignment on shared inscriptions. However, neither available source
provides the comprehensive, archaeologically-provenanced multi-site coverage
that your ICIT corpus offers.
